## Part 2: Market Sizing — TAM / SAM / SOM (30 min)

### Slide 4 — Why Market Sizing Matters
- Investors WILL ask: "How big is this market?"
- More importantly: YOU need to know if the market is big enough to be a business
- **Key mindset:** Big numbers are exciting. Realistic numbers are useful.

### Slide 5 — The Three Rings Model
```
       TAM (Total Addressable Market)
    ┌─────────────────────────────┐
    │     SAM (Serviceable Market)│
    │  ┌──────────────────────┐   │
    │  │   SOM (Serviceable   │   │
    │  │   Obtainable Market) │   │
    │  │                      │   │
    │  └──────────────────────┘   │
    └─────────────────────────────┘
```

### Slide 6 — TAM: Total Addressable Market
- **Definition:** The total global market for your product or service
- **If you had ZERO competition and unlimited resources, how many people might want this?**
- **Method:** Number of potential customers × Average annual spend
- **Example — Bubble tea shop:**
  - Number of bubble tea drinkers in the world: 200M+
  - Average annual spend: ~\$200
  - TAM = 200M × \$200 = \$40 Billion
- **What it tells you:** "Is this a real market?" (If TAM is tiny, it's a hobby, not a business)

### Slide 7 — SAM: Serviceable Available Market
- **Definition:** The portion of TAM you can reach with your specific offering
- **Where do you actually operate?** (Singapore, Southeast Asia, or global?)
- **Example — Bubble tea shop in Singapore:**
  - SG bubble tea drinkers: ~2M
  - Average annual spend: ~\$150 (Singapore prices)
  - SAM = 2M × \$150 = \$300M
- **What it tells you:** "Is the specific market I'm entering worth it?"

### Slide 8 — SOM: Serviceable Obtainable Market
- **Definition:** The market share you can realistically capture
- **Based on:** Your capacity, competition, marketing reach, differentiation
- **Example — ONE bubble tea shop in a mall:**
  - Foot traffic: 500,000/year passing by
  - Conversion: 3% buy bubble tea
  - Average spend: \$6
  - SOM = 500,000 × 3% × \$6 × 1 visit = \$90,000
- **What it tells you:** "Can I make a living from this?"

### Slide 9 — TAM / SAM / SOM — Singapore Startup Examples
| Company | TAM | SAM | SOM (early) |
|---------|-----|-----|------------|
| Carousell | Global classifieds: \$100B+ | SG + SEA classifieds: \$5B | SG marketplace users: \$50M |
| Doctor Anywhere | Global telemedicine: \$250B+ | SG telemedicine: \$200M | SG insured customers: \$5M |
| Your team | ? | ? | ? |

**Key insight:** If SOM < \$100K/year, it's a side project, not a business. If SOM > \$10M/year, you have a real opportunity.

### Slide 10 — Market Sizing Workshop (15 min)
**In teams:** Calculate TAM / SAM / SOM for your team's problem

**Template:**
1. Who has this problem? (total in world) × (annual spend) = **TAM**
2. Who can I reach? (SG or region) × (annual spend) = **SAM**
3. What can I capture? (capacity-based estimate) = **SOM**

**Examples:**
- **Problem:** Students struggle to find affordable lunch near campus
  - TAM: All university students × \$500/year = massive
  - SAM: SIT students × \$500 = ~\$4M
  - SOM: Even 5% of SIT students = ~\$200K

**Debrief:** Which teams have a viable SOM? Which need to rethink?

## Part 3: Competitive Analysis (20 min)

### Slide 11 — Direct vs. Indirect Competitors
- **Direct:** Same problem, same solution (e.g., GrabFood vs. Foodpanda)
- **Indirect:** Same problem, different solution (e.g., GrabFood vs. cooking at home)
- **Replacement:** Different problem, same budget (e.g., GrabFood vs. Netflix subscription)

**Why indirect matters:** Your biggest competitor might not be another startup — it might be the status quo ("they just won't do anything")

### Slide 13 — Blue Ocean Strategy
- **Red Ocean:** Competing in an existing market with many players (bloody competition)
- **Blue Ocean:** Creating a new market where competition is irrelevant
- **Strategy:** Don't be better at the same thing. Be different in a way that matters.

**Examples:**
- **Red Ocean:** Another bubble tea shop → compete on price, location, flavor
- **Blue Ocean:** Bubble tea subscription service → new model entirely
- **Red Ocean:** Another food delivery app → compete on delivery time, fees
- **Blue Ocean:** Pre-ordering from hawker stalls before lunch → new behavior

**Your turn:** What's the blue ocean version of your idea?

### Slide 14 — Feasibility: Go or No-Go? (10 min)
**Decision framework:**
| Factor | Go Signal | Caution Signal | No-Go Signal |
|--------|-----------|----------------|--------------|
| Problem validity | 3+ interviews confirm it | Mixed signals | Interviews show no problem |
| Market size (SOM) | >\$1M/year | \$100K-\$1M | <\$100K/year |
| Competition | Indirect only; no direct | Few direct, well-funded | Many direct, well-funded |
| Your capability | Have skills to build MVP | Need 1-2 partners | Can't build it at all |
| Personal passion | Excited to work on this | Mild interest | No motivation |

**Team exercise:** Rate each factor for your idea. Total score out of 10. Discuss: Should you pursue this or find a new problem?

## Open Discussion Block (15 min)

1. **SOM doubt:** If honest SOM for most student ideas is <\$100K, is it still worth pursuing? Or should you only start if SOM is \$1M+?
2. **Competition avoidance:** Is it better to have no competitors (uncharted territory) or many competitors (validated market)?
3. **Singapore size:** With only 5.6M people, does TAM/SAM/SOM even make sense for Singapore startups? Should you be thinking about SEA from Day 1?
4. **Founder Capability:** If you can't build the solution yourself, should you still pursue the idea? Or find a co-founder?
