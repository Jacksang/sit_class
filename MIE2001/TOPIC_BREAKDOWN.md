# MIE2001 — Topic-by-Topic Teaching Materials Index

## Week 1: What is Entrepreneurship?

**Core question:** What does it mean to be an entrepreneur, and could it be for you?

### Learning Objectives
- Define entrepreneurship beyond "starting a company"
- Identify entrepreneurial mindset traits they already have
- Distinguish between创业者 myths and realities
- Understand the module structure and expectations

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **Opening hook** (5 min): "Name 5 entrepreneurs you admire" — then show they're all different
2. **Defining entrepreneurship** (10 min): 
   - Traditional: Schumpeter's creative destruction
   - Modern: problem-solving + value creation
   - SIT context: applied entrepreneurship
3. **Mindsets** (15 min):
   - Fixed vs. growth mindset (Carol Dweck exercise)
   - Effectuation vs. causation (Saras Sarasvathy's framework)
   - Self-assessment: entrepreneurial orientation quiz
4. **Myths** (10 min):
   - "You need a million-dollar idea" → No, you need a real problem
   - "You need to be a risk-taker" → Good entrepreneurs mitigate risk
   - "You need to be young/tech/rich" → Diverse founder data
5. **Module roadmap** (5 min): what they'll achieve by Week 13

### In-Class Activity (30 min)
**"Pitch a Bad Idea"**
- Each student has 60 seconds to pitch the worst business idea they can think of
- Class votes on: most creative, most likely to accidentally succeed, most entertaining
- Debrief: what made even bad ideas compelling? (confidence, clarity, energy)

### Homework
- 250-word reflection: "One problem I noticed this week that could be a business"
- Read: "Why the Lean Startup Changes Everything" (HBR article)

## Week 2: Opportunity Recognition

**Core question:** How do entrepreneurs find ideas that are actually worth pursuing?

### Learning Objectives
- Train the ability to see problems as opportunities
- Practice systematic opportunity identification
- Learn to distinguish problems from annoyances
- Generate 10+ potential business ideas

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **Where ideas come from** (15 min):
   - Personal experience (scratch your own itch)
   - Industry observation (inefficiencies)
   - Demographic/technology shifts
   - Serendipity (prepare your mind to notice)
2. **The Problem-first approach** (15 min):
   - Jobs-to-be-done framework
   - Problem vs. solution thinking
   - Case: how Grab started from a taxi problem
3. **Evaluating ideas quickly** (15 min):
   - The Napkin Test: is it a problem, market, can you build it?
   - Idea scoring matrix (interest × feasibility × market size)

### In-Class Activity (45 min)
**"Campus Scavenger Hunt"**
- Walk around campus in pairs for 20 min
- Find 10 frustrations/inefficiencies/problems
- Back in class: share top 3, class votes on most promising
- Winners get to work on their idea for the semester

### Homework
- Finalize problem list (top 3 ranked)
- Read: "The Mom Test" Ch 1-2

## Week 3: Customer Discovery

**Core question:** How do you know if anyone actually cares about your idea?

### Learning Objectives
- Learn the difference between customer discovery and sales
- Practice effective customer interview techniques
- Analyze interview data to validate/invalidate assumptions
- Understand the concept of problem-solution fit

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **Why we interview** (10 min):
   - Most startup assumptions are wrong
   - The cost of building something nobody wants
   - Case: Singapore startup that interviewed 100 people before building
2. **The Mom Test principles** (20 min):
   - Don't pitch, don't lead, don't ask "would you buy?"
   - Ask about past behavior, not future intentions
   - Three types of data: facts, emotions, commitments
3. **Interview structure** (15 min):
   - Framing: "I'm learning about [problem area]"
   - Questions: open-ended, specific, behavioral
   - Recording and note-taking

### In-Class Activity (30 min)
**Role-play interviews**
- One student is "founder," one is "customer"
- Scripted scenarios (bad interview vs. good interview)
- Class critiques what worked
- Repeat with different pairs

### Homework
- Interview 5 people about your problem area
- Submit: interview notes + 1-page problem validation report
- Read: "The Mom Test" Ch 3-5

## Week 4: Market Sizing & Analysis

**Core question:** Is this opportunity big enough to be a business?

### Learning Objectives
- Calculate TAM, SAM, SOM for any business idea
- Conduct basic competitive analysis
- Understand when a market is "big enough"
- Identify market trends and inflection points

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **Market sizing basics** (20 min):
   - TAM: total addressable market (top-down)
   - SAM: serviceable available market
   - SOM: serviceable obtainable market (realistic)
   - Examples: calculate for a bubble tea shop vs. SaaS
2. **Competitive analysis** (15 min):
   - Direct vs. indirect competitors
   - Competitive matrix (price, features, positioning)
   - Blue Ocean vs. Red Ocean
3. **When is a market "big enough"?** (10 min):
   - For a lifestyle business vs. venture-backed startup
   - Singapore-specific: small market, global mindset

### In-Class Activity (30 min)
**Market sizing workshop**
- Pick 3 ideas from the class
- Calculate TAM/SAM/SOM for each in groups
- Present and compare results
- Identify which ideas have "escape velocity"

### Homework
- Complete market sizing worksheet for your idea
- Identify top 3 competitors (direct or indirect)
- Read: Blue Ocean Strategy summary (HBR)

## Week 5: Business Model Canvas

**Core question:** How do you turn an idea into a structured business model?

### Learning Objectives
- Understand all 9 building blocks of the Business Model Canvas
- Apply BMC to real companies and their own ideas
- Identify the most critical assumptions in their model
- Understand BMC as a living, iterative tool

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **Introduction to BMC** (15 min):
   - History: Osterwalder's Business Model Generation
   - The 9 blocks overview
   - BMC vs. traditional business plan
2. **Deep dive by blocks** (20 min):
   - Value Proposition, Customer Segments (left side)
   - Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue (middle)
   - Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partners, Cost Structure (right side)
3. **Lean Canvas alternative** (10 min):
   - Problem, Solution, Key Metrics, Unfair Advantage
   - When to use which

### In-Class Activity (45 min)
**BMC poster session**
- Each team gets a large poster + markers
- Draft their BMC on the poster
- Walk around gallery-style, leave sticky note feedback
- Iterate based on feedback

### Homework
- Finalize BMC poster (photo + digital version)
- Identify: which block has the most assumptions?
- Read: Business Model Generation Ch 1-2

## Week 6: Value Proposition & MVP

**Core question:** What exactly are you building, and what's the smallest version that proves it works?

### Learning Objectives
- Design a clear value proposition
- Understand the Value Proposition Canvas
- Define and scope a Minimum Viable Product
- Plan an MVP test

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **Value Proposition Canvas** (15 min):
   - Customer Profile: jobs, pains, gains
   - Value Map: products/services, pain relievers, gain creators
   - Fit: where the two maps align
2. **What is an MVP?** (15 min):
   - The smallest thing you can build to learn
   - Not a prototype, not a beta — it's a learning vehicle
   - Examples: landing page, concierge MVP, Wizard of Oz
3. **Testing your MVP** (15 min):
   - What's the riskiest assumption?
   - Design an experiment to test it
   - Success criteria: what would convince you?

### In-Class Activity (30 min)
**MVP design sprint**
- Each team: identify their riskiest assumption
- Design the cheapest possible MVP to test it
- Sketch the MVP, define success criteria
- Share with class for feedback

### Homework
- Build your MVP (landing page, paper prototype, or whatever is simplest)
- Test with 5-10 potential users
- Document results: what did you learn?
- Read: "The Lean Startup" Ch 6-7

## Week 7: Mid-term Presentations

**Objective:** Present progress, get feedback, course-correct

### Format
- 5-minute presentation per team
- 3 minutes Q&A / feedback
- Total: ~2 hours for 15 teams

### What to Present
1. Problem + customer discovery results (2 min)
2. Business Model Canvas (1 min)
3. MVP + test results (1 min)
4. What you've learned + what changed (1 min)

### Grading Criteria
- **Problem validation:** Did you talk to real customers? (30%)
- **BMC quality:** Are the blocks coherent? (30%)
- **MVP + learning:** Did you test and learn? (25%)
- **Presentation clarity:** Can we understand your idea? (15%)

### Deliverable
- 5-slide pitch deck
- Updated BMC

## Week 8: Revenue Models & Pricing

**Core question:** How will this business actually make money?

### Learning Objectives
- Understand common revenue models for startups
- Choose the right pricing strategy
- Calculate basic unit economics
- Understand the difference between revenue and profit

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **Revenue models** (20 min):
   - Transaction, subscription, freemium, advertising, marketplace
   - Two-sided market dynamics
   - Case: Grab (transaction) vs. Netflix (subscription) vs. Canva (freemium)
2. **Pricing psychology** (15 min):
   - Cost-plus vs. value-based pricing
   - Anchoring, decoy pricing, tiered pricing
   - Singapore consumer price sensitivity
3. **Unit economics basics** (10 min):
   - Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
   - Lifetime Value (LTV)
   - The magic ratio: LTV > 3x CAC

### In-Class Activity (30 min)
**Pricing exercise**
- Given a product concept, teams decide pricing
- Justify with customer research
- Class debates which price is right
- Calculate basic unit economics

### Homework
- Define revenue model for your venture
- Calculate rough CAC and LTV
- Read: case study of a Singapore startup's revenue model

## Week 9: Financial Basics for Entrepreneurs

**Core question:** How do you make a financial plan without being an accountant?

### Learning Objectives
- Build a simple financial model in a spreadsheet
- Understand break-even analysis
- Calculate startup costs and runway
- Know the basics of bootstrapping vs. fundraising

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **Startup costs** (10 min):
   - One-time costs vs. recurring costs
   - Fixed vs. variable costs
   - Singapore-specific: incorporation, rent, salary benchmarks
2. **Revenue projections** (10 min):
   - Top-down vs. bottom-up forecasting
   - The "hockey stick" trap
   - Conservative, realistic, optimistic scenarios
3. **Break-even analysis** (10 min):
   - When do you stop losing money?
   - Contribution margin
   - Runway: how long before you run out of money?
4. **Bootstrapping vs. Fundraising** (15 min):
   - When to raise money vs. when to bootstrap
   - Sources of funding in Singapore (grants, angels, VCs)
   - The dilution trade-off

### In-Class Activity (45 min)
**Financial model building**
- Use provided Google Sheets template
- Fill in: startup costs, monthly expenses, revenue projections
- Calculate break-even point
- If time: create 3 scenarios

### Homework
- Complete financial model in Google Sheets
- Write a paragraph: "How much money do I need to start this business?"
- Read: case study on bootstrapping in Singapore

## Week 10: Go-to-Market Strategy

**Core question:** How do you reach your first customers — especially in Singapore?

### Learning Objectives
- Identify the right channels to reach customers
- Create a marketing plan on a startup budget
- Understand growth loops vs. paid acquisition
- Plan a go-to-market strategy for Singapore

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **Channels** (15 min):
   - Direct sales, online marketing, partnerships, content marketing
   - Bullseye framework (testing channels systematically)
   - Singapore-specific: Telegram, WhatsApp, Carousell, word-of-mouth
2. **Marketing on a budget** (15 min):
   - Growth hacking basics
   - Content marketing for B2B
   - Community-led growth
   - Case: how a Singapore startup got first 100 customers
3. **Go-to-market plan structure** (15 min):
   - Target customer
   - Channel mix
   - Marketing budget
   - Timeline and milestones

### In-Class Activity (30 min)
**Channel brainstorming**
- For each team's venture: brainstorm 10 channels
- Rank by: reach × relevance × cost
- Select top 3 to test
- Design a 30-day acquisition experiment

### Homework
- Write 1-page GTM plan
- Identify: "our first 10 customers will come from..."
- Read: Traction (Gabriel Weinberg) — Bullseye Framework

## Week 11: Pitching & Storytelling

**Core question:** How do you tell your story in a way that makes people want to invest?

### Learning Objectives
- Structure a compelling pitch deck
- Master the art of storytelling for business
- Handle Q&A confidently
- Deliver a clear, concise pitch

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **The pitch deck structure** (15 min):
   - Problem → Solution → Market → Traction → Team → Financials → Ask
   - 10 slides, 20 minutes, 30pt font (Guy Kawasaki's rules)
   - What investors actually look for
2. **Storytelling techniques** (15 min):
   - The hero's journey for startups
   - Data with narrative (not just numbers)
   - Emotional connection + logical argument
3. **Handling Q&A** (15 min):
   - Anticipating tough questions
   - "I don't know" is an acceptable answer (show curiosity)
   - Common investor questions and how to prepare

### In-Class Activity (45 min)
**Pitch practice**
- Each team pitches for 5 minutes
- Class gives structured feedback (2 stars + 1 wish)
- Video record for self-review
- Iterate based on feedback

### Homework
- Refine pitch deck based on feedback
- Record yourself pitching (phone video)
- Self-critique: what would you improve?
- Practice until you can pitch without notes

## Week 12: Building Your Team & Founder Stories

**Core question:** Who do you need on your team, and what can you learn from real founders?

### Learning Objectives
- Understand co-founder dynamics and equity splits
- Learn from real Singapore founder journeys
- Create a team charter for their venture
- Understand startup culture and values

### Lecture Outline (45 min)
1. **Co-founder dynamics** (15 min):
   - Why investors bet on teams, not ideas
   - Co-founder equity: splitting fairly
   - The vesting schedule concept
   - What to look for in a co-founder
2. **Founder stories panel** (20 min) — Guest speakers
   - 2-3 SIT alumni or local founders
   - Each shares: journey, failure, lesson learned
   - Q&A with students
3. **Team charter exercise** (10 min):
   - Roles and responsibilities
   - Decision-making process
   - Conflict resolution protocol

### In-Class Activity (30 min)
**Charter negotiation**
- Before class: each student fills out a "team values" worksheet
- In class: negotiate and agree on a team charter
- Sign it (digital signature)
- This document becomes their operating agreement

### Homework
- Submit signed team charter
- Write: "What I learned from the founders today" (250 words)
- Prepare for final presentations

## Week 13: Final Presentations

**Objective:** Present your venture as if pitching to real investors

### Format
- 7-minute presentation
- 5 minutes Q&A
- Judging panel (instructors + guest judges)

### What to Present
1. Problem & customer validation (1.5 min)
2. Solution & value proposition (1 min)
3. Business Model Canvas highlights (1 min)
4. Traction / MVP results (1 min)
5. Go-to-Market plan (1 min)
6. Team (0.5 min)
7. Financial ask (1 min)

### Deliverable
- **10-slide pitch deck** (PDF)
- **Business Plan** (executive summary + BMC + financial model — 5 pages max)
- **BMC poster** (digital or physical)

### Grading Criteria
| Component | Weight |
|-----------|--------|
| Problem validation | 20% |
| Business model quality | 20% |
| MVP/validation results | 20% |
| Pitch quality | 20% |
| Q&A handling | 10% |
| Teamwork (peer eval) | 10% |
