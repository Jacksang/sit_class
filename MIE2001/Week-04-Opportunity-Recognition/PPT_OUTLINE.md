## Part 2: Sources of Opportunity (25 min)

### Slide 4 — Where Do Business Opportunities Come From?
**4 major sources:**
1. Technology shifts
2. Demographic changes
3. Industry inefficiencies
4. Personal experience

### Slide 5 — Source 1: Technology Shifts
- New technologies create new possibilities
- **Current waves:** AI, blockchain, IoT, biotech, clean energy
- **Singapore examples:**
  - AI → Better.sg (AI for mental health)
  - Biotech → MiRXES (RNA diagnostics from Singapore)
  - Clean energy → Sunseap (solar in SG)
- **Student opportunity:** How can you apply these technologies to solve a Singapore problem?
- **Caution:** Don't start with the technology — start with the problem, then see if tech can help

### Slide 6 — Source 2: Demographic Changes
- Aging population → senior care, health tech, retirement planning
- Gig economy → freelance tools, job platforms, insurance
- Digital natives → new education, entertainment, communication needs
- **Singapore-specific:**
  - Fastest aging population in Asia → elderly services
  - High foreign workforce → integration services, recruitment
  - Rising singles → new housing, travel, social products

**Discussion:** What demographic trend affects you most directly?

### Slide 7 — Source 3: Industry Inefficiencies
- Fragmented markets → consolidation opportunity
- Outdated processes → digitalization opportunity
- Poor customer service → experience improvement opportunity
- **Singapore examples:**
  - Food delivery: fragmented hawker centers → GrabFood unified them
  - Parking: disconnected parking lots → Parking.sg unified the experience
  - Banking: complex forms → simple digital banking (GXS, Trust Bank)

**Ask yourself:** "What's an industry that still works the way it did 20 years ago?"

### Slide 8 — Source 4: Personal Experience (Scratch Your Own Itch)
- Some of the best businesses solve the founder's own problem
- **Examples:**
  - Warby Parker: founder lost his glasses and couldn't afford replacements
  - Carousell: founders were tired of cluttered classifieds
  - You: what's a problem YOU face regularly that no one has solved well?
- **Why this works:** You ARE the target customer. You have the deepest empathy.

### Slide 9 — The Problem-First Approach
**Wrong order:** "I want to start a business. What's a hot industry? I'll build an app for that."

**Right order:** "I see a real problem people have. I understand it deeply. Now... what business could solve it?"

**The filter:**
1. Is this problem real? (people feel pain)
2. Is it frequent? (happens regularly, not once)
3. Is it intense? (people would pay to solve it)
4. Is it broad? (enough people have it to be a business)

## Part 4: Customer Discovery — The Mom Test (30 min)

### Slide 12 — What is Customer Discovery?
- **Definition:** The process of talking to potential customers to validate that a problem exists and is worth solving
- **Before you build anything, you need to know:**
  1. Does this problem actually exist?
  2. Do people want it solved?
  3. Will they pay for a solution?

### Slide 13 — The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)
- **The problem:** People lie to make you feel good (especially your mom)
- **The solution:** Ask questions that reveal truth, not flattery
- **Core principle:** Talk about their life and problems, not your idea

### Slide 14 — Bad Questions vs. Good Questions
| ❌ Bad (Asking about your idea) | ✅ Good (Asking about their life) |
|----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| "Would you use an app that..." | "Tell me about the last time you [problem]" |
| "Do you think this is a good idea?" | "What do you do when [problem] happens?" |
| "How much would you pay for this?" | "Have you tried to solve this before? What did you try?" |
| "Isn't this frustrating?" | "What's the most frustrating part of [situation]?" |

### Slide 15 — The Three C's of Good Interviews
1. **Context:** Understand their situation and behavior before your idea
2. **Concrete stories:** "Tell me about the LAST time..." (not "usually..." or "in general...")
3. **Commitment signals:** Would they: Give time? Give money? Give introductions?

**Pro tip:** A real commitment is: "Yes, email me when you launch." A fake signal is: "Great idea!"

### Slide 16 — Interview Role-Play (15 min)
**Setup:** One person plays "founder," one plays "interviewee"

**Scenario:** You believe students struggle to find affordable nearby lunch options on campus.

**Founder's goals:**
- Don't pitch your idea
- Ask about their lunch habits
- Discover if this is a real problem
- Get a concrete story (last time they had trouble finding lunch)

**Round 1 (3 min):** Bad interview — ask leading questions
**Round 2 (3 min):** Good interview — open-ended, problem-focused

**Debrief:**
- How did the "good" interview feel different?
- Did you learn more?
- What did the "interviewee" reveal that surprised you?

## Open Discussion Block (15 min)

1. **Scratching your own itch:** Is a founder's personal problem always the right starting point? What if you're not the "average" customer?
2. **Interview anxiety:** What's the hardest part of talking to strangers about their problems? How do you overcome it?
3. **The Mom Test anxiety:** If even your mom will lie to you, how do you know when someone is telling the truth?
4. **Singapore cultural barriers:** In Singapore, people are polite and don't like to say "no" directly. How does this affect customer discovery? How do you work around it?
