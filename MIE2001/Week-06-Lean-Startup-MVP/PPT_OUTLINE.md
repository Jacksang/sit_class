## Part 2: The Lean Startup Philosophy (20 min)

### Slide 4 — The Problem with Traditional Product Development
- Traditional: Write spec → Build → Launch → Hope people want it
- Result: Months of work, \$ wasted on wrong product
- **Alternative:** Build just enough to test assumptions → Learn → Iterate

### Slide 5 — Build-Measure-Learn Loop
```
     ┌──── Build ────┐
     │               ▼
  Learn          Measure
     ▲               │
     └───────────────┘
```

- **Build:** Create the smallest thing that tests an assumption
- **Measure:** Collect data from real users
- **Learn:** Did your assumption hold? Pivot or persevere?
- **Goal:** Go around the loop as fast as possible

### Slide 6 — Validated Learning
- **Definition:** Evidence-based progress, not opinion-based
- **Opinion:** "I think students would use a meal-planning app"
- **Validated learning:** "We interviewed 10 students. 7 said they'd use it. 3 pre-registered with email."
- **Key shift:** Learning > Building. A failed test that teaches you something is better than a successful launch with no learning.

### Slide 7 — Zappos Example (Classic Lean Startup)
- **Step 1:** Assumption: "Will people buy shoes online without trying them?"
- **Step 2 (Build):** Took photos at a local shoe store, posted them online
- **Step 3 (Measure):** People actually ordered!
- **Step 4 (Learn):** Yes, people will buy shoes online — but returns policy matters
- **Result:** Built full business based on validated learning
- **Cost of test:** A few hours and a trip to the mall
- **Alternative cost:** \$500K to build a full e-commerce platform

### Slide 8 — Singapore Examples
- **Carousell MVP:** A simple app that let people sell their old items — no payments, no shipping (they met in person)
- **Grab MVP:** A dispatch system for taxis — no app, no payments (SMS-based)
- **HonestBee:** Started with a simple Google Form + WhatsApp group for honey delivery

**Pattern:** Every successful Singapore startup started simpler than you'd expect.

## Part 3: What is an MVP? (20 min)

### Slide 9 — MVP = Minimum Viable Product
- **NOT** a prototype (not for showing investors)
- **NOT** a beta (not feature-incomplete but ready to launch)
- **NOT** a first version (it's a learning vehicle)
- **Definition:** The smallest thing you can build/do to test your riskiest assumption

### Slide 10 — Types of MVPs (With Examples)
| Type | What You Do | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Landing Page** | Create a page describing your product, see who clicks "sign up" | Buffer started with a landing page |
| **Concierge MVP** | Manually deliver the service yourself | Zappos (buying shoes at the store) |
| **Wizard of Oz** | Users think it's automated, but you're doing it manually | Mailchimp started as Wizard of Oz |
| **Paper Prototype** | Sketch the interface, walk users through it | Early Dropbox video |
| **Single-feature MVP** | Build one feature that tests the core assumption | Foursquare started with just check-ins |
| **Pre-order MVP** | Sell before you build | Kickstarter campaigns |

### Slide 11 — How to Choose Your MVP Type
**Decision tree:**
1. Is your riskiest assumption about demand? → Landing page or Pre-order MVP
2. Is it about service quality? → Concierge MVP
3. Is it about usability? → Paper prototype or Single-feature MVP
4. Is it about feasibility? → Wizard of Oz or Single-feature MVP

### Slide 12 — Common MVP Mistakes
| ❌ Mistake | ✅ Better Approach |
|-----------|-------------------|
| "Our MVP needs a login system" | Start without authentication |
| "We need both iOS and Android" | Pick ONE platform |
| "The app needs to look polished" | Ugly but functional is fine |
| "We need payments first" | Accept manual payments (PayLah, bank transfer) |
| "The MVP is 80% of the full product" | That's not an MVP — that's a full product |

## Part 4: Identifying the Riskiest Assumption (20 min)

### Slide 13 — What is a Riskiest Assumption?
- **Definition:** The assumption that, if wrong, would kill your business
- **NOT** "people might not like the color scheme"
- **YES** "people don't actually have this problem" or "people won't pay"

### Slide 14 — Assumption Mapping
| Assumption Type | Example | How to Test |
|----------------|---------|-------------|
| Problem exists | "Students struggle to find affordable lunch" | Interviews, observation |
| Willingness to pay | "Students will pay \$5/month for meal recommendations" | Pre-orders, landing page sign-ups |
| Solution works | "The app can recommend lunch in under 3 seconds" | Paper prototype, single-feature build |
| You can deliver | "We can negotiate deals with 50 hawkers" | Concierge: manually negotiate with 3 |
| Market size viable | "10,000 students will use this" | Surveys, pre-registrations |

### Slide 15 — Activity: Identify Your Riskiest Assumption (15 min)
**Team exercise:**
1. Write down all your assumptions about your business (brain dump, 3 min)
2. For each, ask: "If this is wrong, does my business still work?"
3. Rank by: Impact if wrong (1-5) × How unsure you are (1-5)
4. The highest score = your riskiest assumption

**Share:** Each team states their riskiest assumption in one sentence.

## Part 5: MVP Design Sprint (45 min)

### Slide 16 — MVP Design Sprint: Instructions
**Goal:** Design an MVP that tests your riskiest assumption

**Time:** 45 min total

**Steps:**
1. Define: What ONE question does your MVP need to answer? (5 min)
2. Design: What's the smallest test that answers it? (10 min)
3. Sketch: Draw/write out your MVP (15 min)
4. Success criteria: What result would convince you your assumption is true? (10 min)
5. Share: 1-min pitch to class (5 min)

### Slide 17 — Step 1: The One Question
**Template:** "By [date], I want to know: [the ONE thing]"

**Examples:**
- "By next Friday, I want to know: Will 20 people sign up for a meal-planning service?"
- "By next class, I want to know: Do our target users confirm this is the #1 frustration?"
- "By Week 7, I want to know: Will 5 people pay for this service?"

### Slide 18 — Step 2: Design the Smallest Test
**For demand questions:** Landing page (create on Carrd/Canva) + share link
**For service questions:** Offer the service manually to 5 people
**For usability questions:** Paper prototype + walkthrough with 5 people

**Guiding principle:** If your test costs more than \$50 or takes more than 1 week, it's too big.

### Slide 19 — Step 3: Sketch Your MVP
- Draw your landing page, flow diagram, or service script
- Include: what the user sees, what happens next, what you measure
- **Low fidelity is fine** — stick figures and scribbles OK

### Slide 20 — Step 4: Define Success Criteria
**Bad success criteria:** "Lots of people sign up"
**Good success criteria:** "At least 20 sign-ups from a targeted Instagram post within 3 days"

**Framing:**
- What's the minimum result that tells you "this assumption is probably true"?
- What result would tell you "this assumption is wrong — pivot needed"?
- What's the "grey zone" result where you need to test more?

### Slide 21 — Team Shares (10 min)
- **Each team:** 90 seconds to present:
  1. Riskiest assumption
  2. MVP design
  3. Success criteria
