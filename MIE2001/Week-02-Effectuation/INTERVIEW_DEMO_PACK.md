### ⭐ The Opening Hook (First 90 Seconds) — *This is critical*

**Do NOT start with:** "Today we'll learn about effectuation principles..."

**Start with this:**

> *(Stand comfortably, no notes)*
>
> "I want you to imagine something. You're given \$5,000 and told to start a business in two weeks. What do you do?
>
> Most of you — and I mean *most MBA students, most first-year entrepreneurs* — will do the same thing: research the market, write a plan, forecast revenue. That's called *causation*. Plan first, execute later.
>
> But here's the thing Saras Sarasvathy discovered by interviewing *actual* expert entrepreneurs — people who've built multiple successful companies. They don't start with a plan.
>
> They start with a question: *'What do I have right now?'*
>
> That one question — *'what do I have'* — is the seed of something called **effectuation**. And it's the foundation of how real entrepreneurs think, especially right here in Singapore.

**Why this works:**
- Immediately hooks with a concrete scenario
- Creates tension (you'd probably do it wrong)
- Introduces the core concept through *contrast* (causation vs. effectuation)
- Landmine reference to Singapore (shows local relevance)
- Takes < 90 seconds

### Detailed Script for Each Segment

#### Segment 1: Hook (0:00-1:30)
*See above*

#### Segment 2: Sarasvathy's Discovery (1:30-4:00)

> "Saras Sarasvathy — a professor at UVA Darden — did something fascinating. She took 27 expert entrepreneurs — founders with 15+ years of experience, multiple exits — and gave them a standardized problem. She asked: *'How would you build a venture for this product?'*
>
> Then she compared their thinking to MBA students and corporate managers.
>
> The result? Expert entrepreneurs don't think causally — *'what goal do I want to achieve, then what resources do I need?'* — they think **effectually**: *'what resources do I have, then what can I create with them?'*
>
> That's the paradigm shift this course is built on. And it breaks down into **five principles** that you can literally apply today."
>
> *(Briefly flash all 5 on screen — 10 seconds — then zoom into the first two)*

#### Segment 3: Bird-in-Hand (4:00-7:00)

> "Principle one: **Bird-in-Hand**.
>
> 'Start with who you are, what you know, and who you know.'
>
> Sounds simple. But think about how counterintuitive this is. Every business textbook tells you: do market research first. Analyze the competition. Raise capital. *Then* start.
>
> Expert entrepreneurs do the opposite. They ask: *What do I already have?*
>
> **Singapore case:** Carousell. Three friends — Siu Rui, Marcus, Lucas. They didn't start with venture capital. They started with:
> - **Who they are:** mobile developers and designers
> - **What they know:** marketplace UX from previous projects
> - **Who they know:** NUS classmates, Singtel contacts
>
> They built the first version in a weekend. Because they weren't starting from zero. They were starting from *what they had*.
>
> *(Pause)*
>
> *Now, to you.* What if I asked you right now: *What do you have?* Take 30 seconds — think about one resource you're sitting on right now that you haven't considered.

#### Segment 4: Affordable Loss (7:00-10:00)

> "But what about risk? 'Bird-in-Hand sounds good, but what if I lose everything I have?'
>
> That brings us to principle two: **Affordable Loss**.
>
> Most people think entrepreneurs are fearless risk-takers. That's a myth. Expert entrepreneurs are actually *very* careful. But they calculate risk differently.
>
> **The traditional approach:** 'How much could I *make*?' Project millions. Get excited. Overspend.
>
> **The effectual approach:** 'What's the *most* I could *lose*?' Calculate the downside. Only invest what you can survive losing.
>
> **Example:** Dyson made 5,126 failed prototypes before the first working vacuum. But each prototype cost a small, survivable amount. He didn't bet the farm on prototype #1. He bet small, learned, iterated.
>
> *(Turn to audience)*
>
> *For a student in Singapore:* What's YOUR affordable loss?
> - Money: Maybe \$200 you can spend testing an idea?
> - Time: 5 hours a week you can commit without grades slipping?
> - Reputation: Are you willing to look foolish?
>
> *Here's the magic:* When you define your affordable loss, you're suddenly *free* to take risks — because you've already capped the downside."

#### Segment 5: Interactive — My Effectual Profile (10:00-13:00)

> "Let's make this real for you. Take out your phone or a piece of paper.
>
> *(Project the worksheet)*
>
> I want you to answer just **two questions** — 60 seconds each:
>
> **Question 1 — Bird-in-Hand:** *'What's one skill, one connection, or one resource you have right now that could be the starting point of a venture?'*
>
> *(Give 60 seconds — count down aloud at 30s and 10s)*
>
> **Question 2 — Affordable Loss:** *'What's the most you could invest — money, time, or ego — without it hurting?'*
>
> *(Give 60 seconds)*
>
> *(Ask 1-2 people to share — 60 seconds each)*
>
> *(Facilitator insight:)* "Notice: nobody said 'I have nothing.' Nobody said 'I can't take any risk.'"
>
> *That's the power of effectuation. It reframes 'I'm not ready' into 'I already have what I need as a starting point.'*

#### Segment 6: Closing & Takeaway (13:00-18:00)

> *(Summarize with energy)*
>
> "We covered two principles today. But here's what I want you to remember — not five bullet points, but ONE shift in thinking:
>
> *(Pause. Look at the committee.)*
>
> **Don't ask 'what do I need?' Ask 'what do I have?'**
>
> That one question changes everything. It turns 'I'm not ready' into 'Let me start.' It turns analysis paralysis into action.
>
> And action — as we'll see in Week 3 with Design Thinking — is the only thing that teaches you what actually works.
>
> Thank you."

## 2. Key Talking Points for Q&A

The committee will likely ask about your teaching philosophy, methodology, and why you designed Week 2 this way. Prepare these:

### On the "Two-Deep" Approach
> "I chose to go deep on **just two principles** (Bird-in-Hand, Affordable Loss) rather than all five. Reason: students remember *one* insight they internalize better than *five* they skim. The remaining three principles are covered in the full lecture and lab activity. This is 'spiral learning' — introduce, apply, reinforce."

### On the Singapore Focus
> "Every principle is grounded in a Singapore case — Carousell, Doctor Anywhere, Grab. International research (Sarasvathy's work) is valid, but students learn best when they can see themselves in the story. A student at SIT can think: *'If Carousell's founders started from their HDB flat, why can't I?'*"

### On Interactive Learning
> "The 60-second worksheet exercise isn't filler. It converts abstract theory into personal relevance. When a student writes down *their* affordable loss, the principle becomes *their* insight — not something they'll forget after the exam."

### On Assessment Alignment
> "The weekly reflection asks students to apply one effectuation principle to a real decision. This tests **LO1** directly — 'Interpret the effectual entrepreneurship principles' — not through memorization, but through real application."

### On Removing Midterm/Final Presentations
> "By removing class-time presentations, I freed up 3 weeks for deeper content delivery without reducing substantive assessment. The final Business Plan is a written submission — it demands the same synthesis work, just without the stage performance anxiety that some students find distracting."

## 3. The "Hook" Strategy — Deeper Principles

For the interview, the committee is evaluating **you as an educator**, not just the content. Here's what they're looking for:

| What They Observe | How You Demonstrate It |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Organization** | Clear structure: Hook → Teach → Apply → Land |
| **Content mastery** | You know the research (Sarasvathy name-check, methodology) |
| **Engagement instincts** | You turn content *into an experience* (live worksheet) |
| **Singapore relevance** | Every example is local, every case hits home |
| **Student empathy** | You acknowledge the student's real constraints (time, money, fear) |
| **Confidence with silence** | You pause; you're not afraid of unfilled space |
| **Flexibility** | You adjust based on audience reaction (genuine, not robotic) |

### The "Living Case" Technique
When you tell a story like Carousell's founding, deliver it **like a story**, not a slide. Stand away from the lectern. Make eye contact. Use your hands. The committee needs to see you can *command a room*, not just read a slide.

## 4. Potential Committee Questions — With Answers

### Q1: "Why effectuation for first-year business students? Isn't this too advanced?"

> "Great question. Actually, effectuation is *perfect* for first-year students — more so than for MBAs. Here's why:
>
> First-years don't have resources — no money, no team, no company. And effectuation says: *that's fine. Start with what you have.*
>
> A first-year SIT student HAS: their course knowledge, their classmates, their network, their curiosity, their weekends. Effectuation gives them permission to start *now* rather than waiting until they graduate.
>
> Causation thinking — 'make a plan, raise capital, hire a team' — paralyzes beginners. Effectuation empowers them."

### Q2: "You only covered two principles in your demo. How do you cover all five?"

> "The full Week 2 class is 3 hours. The demo captured the *deep dive* portion — about 45 minutes of lecture. In the actual class:
>
> - We complete all 5 principles in the lecture portion (45 min)
> - Then students complete the **My Effectual Profile** worksheet individually (15 min)
> - Then they apply all 5 to a Singapore startup case in groups (15 min)
> - Then they do a **speed networking** activity where they share their profiles (15 min)
> - There's also a **10-minute unit test** to check comprehension
>
> So my 18-minute demo was purpose-built to show *depth*, not breadth. In the full class, they get both."

### Q3: "How do you assess whether students truly understand effectuation?"

> "Three layers:
>
> 1. **Immediate:** The in-class unit test (10 min, multiple choice + application) gives me real-time feedback on who's confused
>
> 2. **Weekly:** The reflection asks them to apply one principle to a decision they made. I don't grade 'correctness' — I grade *genuine application*. Did they try? Did they struggle? That's learning.
>
> 3. **Long-term:** The Customer Discovery Report (Week 6) and Business Model Canvas (Week 9) both require students to demonstrate effectual thinking — for example, their MVP should reflect Affordable Loss, not speculative investment."

### Q4: "This looks like a heavy interactive format. How do you handle large classes?"

> "A few techniques:
>
> - The 'My Effectual Profile' worksheet can be done on paper (no tech needed)
> - Pair sharing replaces whole-class discussion for speed
> - The unit test is multiple choice — self-marked in 2 minutes
> - The case study analysis uses a structured table (just 5 rows), so groups produce consistent, comparable output
> - I scan the room during individual work; I can identify struggling students quickly
>
> Interactive doesn't mean chaotic. It means intentionally structured participation."

### Q5: "Why did you move from 13 weeks to 10 weeks? What's lost?"

> "What's gained is more important than what's lost.
>
> **Lost:** Two class sessions — the midterm informal pitch replaces the graded midterm, which was essentially a rehearsal anyway. The final presentation is now a written Business Plan.
>
> **Gained:** Students who dread public speaking get assessed on their *thinking*, not their *stage presence*. And the written Business Plan requires deeper synthesis than a 7-minute pitch can deliver.
>
> The trade-off is deliberate: less performance pressure, more substance."

### Q6: "How do you handle students who claim they have 'nothing' to start with?"

> "Every student at SIT has something. They're in a degree program, at a university with an Innovation & Entrepreneurship office, surrounded by classmates with diverse skills. That's their network. That's their identity.
>
> If a student says 'I have nothing,' I use the **Bird-in-Hand** exercise differently for them. I say: 'OK, list everything you did in the last 24 hours. Every interaction. Every app you opened. Every frustration you felt.' Suddenly they realize they're surrounded by potential starting points.
>
> It's a coaching moment, not a content gap."

### Minimalist Slide Design Rule

- **No paragraphs.** One sentence per slide maximum.
- **Images > text.** Carousell logo. Sarasvathy portrait. Dyson vacuum silhouette.
- **Big numbers only.** "5,126 failed prototypes" — that's a visual.
- **Speaker notes have the detail.** Slides are visual anchors.

The committee should *listen* to you, not read your slides.

## 6. Practice Checklist

Before the interview, run through this checklist:

- [ ] **Hook:** Can I deliver the \$5,000 story without notes in under 90 seconds?
- [ ] **Sarasvathy name:** Do I pronounce it correctly? (*Sah-RAHS-vah-thee*)
- [ ] **Carousell story:** Can I tell the founding story like a narrative, not a list?
- [ ] **Affordable Loss calculation:** Can I ask the audience to calculate and then *anticipate* their answers?
- [ ] **Live worksheet:** If no one volunteers to share, do I have a backup anecdote ready?
- [ ] **Q&A readiness:** Have I rehearsed answers to Questions 1, 3, 4, 5, and 6 above?
- [ ] **Slide-free opening:** Am I comfortable standing *away* from the podium for the first 90 seconds?
- [ ] **Timing:** Does the full demo run in 17-19 minutes (not 22)?
- [ ] **Singapore context:** Do all examples reference Singaporean companies or student life?
- [ ] **Landing:** Does the closing phrase feel natural, or does it sound memorized?
