## Part 2: The 5 Principles (45 min)

### Slide 4 — Sarasvathy's Discovery
- **How she did it:** Interviewed 27 expert entrepreneurs (founders with 15+ years experience)
- **Key finding:** Expert entrepreneurs think differently than novices or managers
- 5 principles emerged — let's learn them

### Slide 5 — Principle 1: Bird-in-Hand
> "Start with who you are, what you know, and who you know"

**Tagline:** Don't wait for the "right" resources. Start with what you have.

**Examples:**
- **Airbnb:** Started when two designers rented air mattresses during a conference — they had a spare room and an air pump
- **Carousell:** Founders had a background in photography and marketplace UX — started with a simple app in Singapore
- **Your story:** What do YOU have right now? (SIT student, Singapore-based, access to professors and peers)

**Class question:** What resource have you been overlooking?

### Slide 6 — Principle 2: Affordable Loss
> "Invest only what you can afford to lose"

**Tagline:** Don't calculate expected returns. Calculate maximum downside you can stomach.

**The shift:**
- Traditional: "How much could I make?" → projected millions
- Effectual: "What's the most I could lose?" → small, survivable amount

**Examples:**
- **Student startup:** \$500 from savings, 2 weekends of time — that's the affordable loss
- **Grab:** Anthony Tan's affordable loss was his time and reputation; he didn't quit his family business immediately
- **Dyson:** 5,126 failed prototypes — but each failure was survivable

**Student calculation:**
- Money: \$________ (what I can spend without hardship)
- Time: ________ hours/week (what I can commit without grades suffering)
- Reputation: What's the worst that happens if people say "that idea is stupid"?

### Slide 7 — Principle 3: Crazy Quilt
> "Build partnerships, don't compete — even with strangers"

**Tagline:** Who can I work with? (Not: who can I beat?)

**Examples:**
- **Shopee:** Partnered with local logistics companies instead of building their own delivery network
- **Carousell:** Partnered with Singtel for user acquisition
- **SIT students:** Can you partner with: SIT Innovation & Entrepreneurship Office? A professor? Another team with complementary skills?

**Activity prompt:** Think of 5 people/organizations who could help you. Write them down. Now... how would you ask them?

### Slide 8 — Principle 4: Lemonade (Lemonade Principle)
> "Embrace surprises — turn bad news into opportunity"

**Tagline:** When life gives you lemons, make lemonade.

**Examples:**
- **Post-it Notes:** Failed super-strong glue → turned into reusable adhesive
- **Slack:** Failed video game (Glitch) → team built internal chat tool → Slack
- **Singapore context:** COVID forced F&B to pivot to delivery → some discovered they made MORE profit on delivery
- **Student example:** Your experiment failed? Now you know something you didn't before. That's valuable.

**Reframe:** "Surprises are not problems to avoid. They are opportunities to discover."

### Slide 9 — Principle 5: Pilot-in-the-Plane
> "Focus on what you can control, not on predicting the future"

**Tagline:** Don't forecast. Act.

**Examples:**
- **Causation perspective:** "I predict the smartwatch market will grow to X billion by 2026 — I should enter"
- **Effectuation perspective:** "I can't control the market. I CAN control: building an MVP this week, talking to 5 people, sending 10 emails"
- **Grab:** Instead of predicting if the taxi industry would accept them, they focused on building a better dispatch system and signing ONE driver at a time

**Key question:** What's ONE thing you can do TODAY instead of worrying about predictions?

## Part 3: Case Study — Singapore Startup (20 min)

### Slide 10 — Case: Pick Any Singapore Startup
**Options to choose from (instructor selects 1-2):**

**Option A: Carousell**
- Started in Singapore (2012) by three friends
- Founders: Quek Siu Rui, Marcus Tan, Lucas Ngoo
- **Bird-in-Hand:** They had mobile development and design skills
- **Affordable Loss:** Small savings, built in weekends
- **Crazy Quilt:** Partnered with Singtel, StarHub for growth
- **Lemonade:** When growth slowed, pivoted to classifieds + payments
- **Pilot-in-the-Plane:** Focused on user acquisition, not market predictions

**Option B: Doctor Anywhere**
- Founded by Wai Mun Wong (2017)
- **Bird-in-Hand:** Founder had healthcare and tech background
- **Crazy Quilt:** Partnered with insurance companies, clinics
- **Lemonade:** COVID → massive growth in telemedicine
- **Pilot-in-the-Plane:** Built one feature at a time, not a full platform

**Group work (10 min):** In teams of 3-4, identify which effectuation principles are visible. Mark each principle and write one sentence of evidence.

**Class debrief (10 min):** Share findings. **Which principle was easiest to identify? Which was hardest?**

## Open Discussion Block (15 min)

### Discussion Questions
1. **Affordable Loss vs. Ambition:** If students only invest what they can afford to lose, does that limit their ambition? Or is it the secret to taking more risk?

2. **Crazy Quilt in Practice:** Have you ever built a partnership that surprised you? (e.g., study group that turned into a project, a contact that opened a door)

3. **Lemonade:** Think of a recent unexpected event in your life. Could you reframe it as an opportunity?

4. **Singapore vs. US Startup Culture:** In the US, "move fast and break things" is celebrated. In Singapore, risk is more calculated. Which approach aligns better with effectuation?
